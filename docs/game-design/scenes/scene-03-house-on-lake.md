## Core Mechanic

**The Memory.** Player inhabits the child body and participates in the lakeside activity with young dad. The activity plays out to its natural conclusion — it is not a puzzle, it is a presence.

The memory has a finite duration or completion state. When it ends, the scene transitions to Scene 4.

## Discoverable Mechanic — The Phone (Recovered)

The phone the player had at the menu — the one that disappeared in Scene 1 — reappears here, in the player's pocket, during a childhood memory. This is memory/dream logic: the present-day device exists anachronistically in the past.

**Discovery:** The player reaches into their pocket (or checks their inventory, or a natural interaction prompt appears near a meaningful moment) and finds the phone there. Not announced. Not prompted. [TBD: what specific interaction or moment causes the player to check their pocket? Is it tied to a particularly beautiful beat in the activity with young dad?]

**What it does:** The player can point the phone at a moment in the scene and photograph it. The snapshot is preserved and carried forward to Scene 5.

**Emotional weight:** The phone was lost when the father was physically present (Scene 1). It is found in a memory of the father when he was young. The device that felt like a barrier to presence in Scene 1 becomes the tool for preserving a connection that cannot otherwise survive time.

**Dissonance note:** This is Scene 3's one permitted dissonance element — a present-day phone appearing in a childhood memory. No other reality-breaking details in this scene.

**Inventory rule:**
- One snapshot per playthrough per scene
- Sequential unlock: a second photographable moment in the same scene accessible only after the first has been taken (potentially across playthroughs)
- Multiple memory moments exist in the scene — discovering all is a replayability goal

## Scenario

The player arrives as a child at the lake. Young dad is there — warm, present, doing something with water. [TBD: fishing? Kayaking? Both are mentioned as options. One per V1, others deferred.]

The activity unfolds. The player participates as the child — following, joining, maybe doing the activity themselves. [TBD: how much agency does the child-player have vs. watching the memory play?]

At some point the player discovers the camera and has the opportunity to photograph a moment. They may discover this on their own — or they may not, and the memory ends without a snapshot.

The memory plays to its natural end. Transition to Scene 4.

**Narrative frame:** This is what the Prodigal Son remembers from before he left — the father's house, the warmth that was there. The lake memory is not idealized nostalgia; it is specific and real. The camera discovery is the player choosing to preserve something, to say "this mattered." The young dad is the same man waiting on the bench in Scene 5 — younger, before whatever happened to put distance between them.

## Design Notes

- The warm palette and activity of this scene must be in strong contrast with Scene 1's cold fog. The player should feel the difference physically — "this is a different kind of world, even though it's the same world."
- The dad recognition arc lives in the visual design here. The young dad should be immediately warm and likeable — so that when the player later remembers the old man from Scene 1, the potential recognition creates cognitive and emotional friction.
- The camera mechanic must be fully discoverable without prompts. [TBD: test this — is there a version where players consistently miss the camera entirely and reach Scene 5 without any snapshot? Is that acceptable? Yes — it leads to Win B or Lose.]
- The child body is a visual statement: the player becomes smaller, the world becomes larger. The father is seen from below. This is what it felt like.
